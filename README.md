# The Funnel Rule

The Funnel Rule is a thinking system that improves decisions by forcing you to consider multiple frames before concluding.

## What this project is

This repository contains:
- A visual explanation of the Funnel Rule
- An interactive tool that lets you test your decision aperture
- A long-term thinking framework I can return to years later

## The Rule (simple version)

The quality of a decision depends on how many frames you open before deciding.

## Why this exists

Most people don’t make bad decisions because they are stupid.
They make bad decisions because they think inside one frame.

This project solves that.

The method is neutral. Output quality is entirely a function of what you pour into it. The more frames you open before reasoning or deciding, the more honest the output. At 100 percent — all relevant frames active — you reach maximum aperture. That is the funnel rule.
The funnel itself does not change. Probability, logic, mathematics — the method stays constant. What changes is the width of the opening. A narrow opening produces a confident-looking output that is actually just the reflection of whichever frames you selected. A wide opening produces an output that has genuinely processed reality from multiple angles and is honest about what it still does not know.
The practical implication is that bias never lives in the method. It lives upstream, in the selection of inputs and frames before the method even begins. This is why a biased analysis can look rigorous. The math was clean. The funnel was working. But only two frames were open so the output was a 33 dressed up as a 95.

Everything you know about a situation goes in at the top. Every perspective. Every piece of evidence. Every angle you can think of. The funnel processes all of it and produces an answer at the bottom.
Now here is the thing most people miss.
The funnel itself is not the problem. The funnel is just logic. The funnel is just math. The funnel works perfectly every single time.
The problem is what you pour in.
If you only pour in one cup of information — say, stories about successful people — the funnel produces a confident answer based on successful people. It looks like truth. It sounds like truth. But it is only as wide as what you poured in.
The more you pour in — failures, opposing views, uncomfortable evidence, contexts you did not consider — the more the answer at the bottom reflects reality rather than just your starting position.
The funnel rule is simply this.
The quality of any answer, prediction, or decision is not determined by how smart the method is. It is determined by how many frames you opened before you started.
One frame in. One frame out. Dressed as certainty.
All frames in. Honest answer out. With honest uncertainty attached.
